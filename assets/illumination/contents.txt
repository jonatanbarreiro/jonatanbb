==============================================================================
 ILLUMINATION OF THE PAGE CONTENTS  (the text, not the logo)
==============================================================================

Companion to bb.txt. bb.txt is about the LOGO —how the two 'b's (and, by the
same points, the annulus) are lit. This file is about the other thing the
illuminating points light: the PAGE COPY —the running text, the headings and
the section rules of the site itself.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 1. TWO MODES OF LIGHT
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A single illuminating point emits light in two distinct ways. Keeping them
separate is the mental model the code is built on:

  GLOWING (atmospheric).  The soft radial glow around a point —and nothing
      more. The glow is direction-free: the REFLECTIVE layer already shows
      exactly where a point lights, so the air around it doesn't need to point
      anywhere. Every point glows radially out to r₋ (the annulus' inner
      radius, its natural reach), with a physical falloff —a point lamp over
      a flat patch, a bright core and a soft tail. The glow is clipped OUT of
      the logo's disc, so no ambient air enters the annulus. The logo is still
      TRACKED by the points, but on one measure: a point's boundary/element
      lighting of the logo is gated by its distance to the annulus —full
      within one r₋ of the boundary, gone at 2·r₋ (the inner DIAMETER: a point
      entering the bowl sees the whole interior). Painted by main.js on the
      cast canvas. Kept at partial intensity, so it doesn't try to carry the
      whole effect.

  REFLECTIVE.  The light actually landing on a surface and lighting it up —the
      page text going gold where a point is near. It is INDEPENDENT of the
      logo: the logo does not block it, and a point close to (or inside) the
      annulus still lights nearby copy. main.js hands this layer the point
      field ungated (each point's full wake w, out to 2·r₋ —twice the glow's
      reach) and reflect.js renders it.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 2. HOW THE REFLECTIVE LAYER WORKS  (reflect.js)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The page copy is real DOM text; it is never replaced or hidden. Over it sits a
page-sized WebGL canvas that ADDS gold where the text is lit. The canvas is
PAGE-ANCHORED —absolutely positioned over the whole page, it scrolls with the
copy in the compositor, so a pure scroll repaints nothing and the gold can
never lag the text (its density yields to the GPU's limits and a pixel budget
on very tall pages):

  - Each WORD (every heading and body word, wrapped in a <span class="litw">) and
    each section RULE (the black .kicker::after bars) is rasterised into a
    coverage mask at its own screen rect. Word rects and baselines come from real
    font metrics and are recached on any reflow (load / font-load / resize /
    browser zoom), so the gold stays registered with the glyphs at every size.
    Registration is also SELF-HEALING: every frame, three sentinel words are
    re-measured against the cache —any drift (a zoom settling, a restored
    scroll, a late font) recaches and re-rasters within that frame. A recache
    always invalidates the raster, and the raster's cache key carries the zoom
    state (dpr + CSS viewport), because zooming in and back out can land on the
    same canvas size with a different world underneath.

  - A fragment shader reads edge normals from that coverage and shades each glyph
    ink -> gold by how lit it is, from the point field (diffuse + a little
    specular, falling off over the point's reach). It is composited OVER the real
    text, so if the GPU ever drops the context the page just shows plain copy —it
    can never go blank— and the section rules and links keep rendering.

  - The raster is PINNED to the DOM, both ends of every word: the canvas is told
    the word's letter-spacing (canvas ignores CSS spacing by default), and
    whatever advance-width quirk remains, the raster is scaled onto the word's
    real DOM width. The gold cannot drift along a word. A word that FRAGMENTS
    across a line break (the breaker takes a dash mid-token as a break point:
    'hello-world' can end a line at the dash) is rasterised per line fragment
    —each fragment gets its own slice of the text at its own rect, never the
    whole-span rect smeared between the lines.

  - The section rules are STRAIGHTENED b's: a bar as thick as the b's ribbon,
    wearing the b's top tip on each end —hook up on the left, hook down on the
    right (the 180° twin, like the monogram's two b's). Lit as three
    superimposed gold pieces —tip / bar / tip— with a hairline seam between
    them, so the joints read subtly, like the b's own middle joint. The straight
    run is rasterised not flat (a flat interior reads as a dead plateau to the
    normal-reading shader) but profiled: full coverage in the middle, soft
    shoulders at the long edges —stroke-like normals along the whole bar.

  - The about-photo FRAME is the same ink, the bars' own material referencing
    the b's: a band one section-bar thick, each side wearing both b-tips hooked
    toward the exterior and, mirrored about the band's midline, toward the
    interior (poking subtly into the photo at the ends). Its dissection is
    TWELVE exact non-overlapping pieces —4 straight runs, cut off where each
    hook ends, and 8 corner PRIMITIVES, each a 7-cornered hook-end extended
    through half the corner square so the pair meets along the corner's full
    45° diagonal (a solid joint). The small notch beyond each outer corner
    stays open. The ink is an SVG built by main.js (frameGeom, shared through
    LITAPI); the lighting is the same twelve pieces —runs profiled across the
    band, primitives solid, hairline seams at the cuts and along each diagonal.

  - The CONTACT ICONS (the Tuta mark, the Telegram plane) are line-art in the
    same ink, so their SVG paths are rasterised into the mask too: a near
    point —or the ignition wave— lights them like everything else.

  - The SELECTION OUTLINE (see §3) is ink too, and rasterised with everything
    else in the photo frame's schematic: mitre-cut sides with corner seams. The
    gold layer sits ABOVE the outline's black divs (the divs live below the GL
    canvas) —with the stack the other way round the box's lighting would be
    painted but never seen.

  - The IGNITION PULSE lights this layer as it travels: the shader carries the
    wave's tent envelope by each pixel's distance to the i-dot —rising from
    the announcer to the main front, draining to half at the requiem and to
    zero a trail later, the same three circumferences the logo reads. While
    rising the light rakes from the i-dot's direction and resolves to a flat,
    direction-free full gold as the front lands (the b elements' own
    treatment), and it is taken as MAX against the steady point light, so the
    wave lifts each glyph over its resting glow and hands back as it leaves.
    Cost: a few shader ops per masked pixel —no extra passes.

  - A visual word split over several spans by inline markup ("Barreiro" is
    Barre|i|ro around the i-dot anchor) is grouped: the pieces share one word
    identity, so it lights, clicks and selects as a single unit.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 3. SELECTION & CLICK
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The reflective idea extends to how text is picked out:

  - Selecting text turns the selected words gold and replaces the native
    selection highlight with a hollow, SHARP-cornered BLACK outline (one box per
    line —roundedness stays reserved for the annulus and the points). Across
    multiple lines only the selection's overall beginning and end are closed;
    an end where the stroke continues on the next line stays open, the box run
    a little past the text to say so.
  - Clicking a word toggles it gold. Either way the whole word turns —a grouped
    word (see above) never lights in parts.
  - Both golds belong to the INTERACTION layer, so the master light (the bulb)
    gates them: lights off, a click toggles nothing, a selection shows only the
    box (words stay ink —the gold is a CSS class the lights-off page mutes),
    and the contact icons don't warm on hover.

The gold is the OFFICIAL site gold #fad02a (--gold): the shade of a clicked
word, of the annulus at max, of a fully lit letter. Partial illumination
everywhere mixes it with black along the warm ember ramp (incRGB), never by
fading gold over the pale page —that path washes the warmth out. The ramp's
low end is kept YELLOW (G tracking R closely), so dim light never leans
brown/bronze or reads olive beside a clicked word's true gold. The GLOWING
layer keeps its own colour (--glow #ffe666, deliberately cooler than the
gold), so the ambient air contrasts with the reflective light.

The illuminating points are the only light sources: a gold word is copy, not a
lamp —it keeps its colour and casts nothing.
